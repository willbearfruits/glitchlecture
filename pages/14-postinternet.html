Post-Internet

2006–2015

Not "after the internet ended" — art made after the internet became the default condition of life.

After the internet is normal

The name trips people up, so let's kill the confusion first. Post-internet does not mean "after the internet ended." Nothing ended. It means art made after the internet became a default condition of life — after being online stopped being a destination you went to and became the water everyone swims in. Once that flips, the old category quietly collapses: art no longer has to happen on the internet to be internet art. The network is no longer a place. It's the climate.

Marisa Olson reached for the word "postinternet" around 2006 to name something specific — work made after one's use of the internet, work that carries the residue of being a heavy user even when it surfaces as a video, a print, or an object in a room. Gene McHugh and Artie Vierkant later picked it up and turned a passing description into a movement-sized term. By the time it had a name, the condition it described was already total.

The image that won't sit still

Artie Vierkant's 2010 essay The Image Object Post-Internet became the load-bearing text. Its argument: an image is no longer a fixed thing. It's a state in a loop — a digital file, then a physical object, then documentation of that object, then a JPEG of the documentation, then a re-upload, then a screenshot of the re-upload. Every hop is reproduction, every reproduction is circulation, and circulation is now the actual life of the work. The "original" is a fiction you cling to out of habit.

That reframing is why post-internet matters for a glitch lecture. Up to here, glitch has lived inside the file — a corrupted byte, a dropped frame, a sorted run of pixels. Post-internet is where glitch leaves the file and becomes a general cultural condition. You no longer have to break anything. The infrastructure breaks it for you, every time something moves.

Glitch becomes a mood

Here's the turn this whole page is built around. Glitch stops being only a technique and becomes a mood — an atmosphere about what it feels like to live inside systems you didn't design and can't leave. Nobody has to corrupt a file to get the look anymore, because the platforms are running the corruption as a background process.

  • Screenshots and memes that degrade a little more with every share.
  • Low-res circulation — the image that's been through fifteen feeds and looks it.
  • Platform compression and Instagram artifacts as a native texture.
  • YouTube aesthetics, broken-UI-as-style, fake operating systems and desktop ruins.
  • Vaporwave-adjacent nostalgia and a low hum of corporate-tech anxiety.

None of these is a deliberate error. They're the ambient byproduct of using the tools as intended. That's the shift: the failure is no longer something an artist does to a medium — it's the texture the medium hands you for free.

The artists working the condition

A loose, sharp crowd ran this terrain. Cory Arcangel treated consumer hardware and pop-culture detritus as readymades. Petra Cortright made webcam videos and digital paintings that lived natively as files in flux. Jon Rafman mined the strange exhaust of Google Street View and online subcultures. Ryan Trecartin built hyperactive, channel-surfing videos that felt like the feed having a breakdown.

Amalia Ulman ran Excellences & Perfections as a four-month performance staged entirely through an Instagram persona — the platform itself as the medium and the gallery. Molly Soda made the confessional, screen-recorded, webcam intimacy of being-online into the work. And the DIS collective wired the whole thing into fashion, advertising, and the aesthetics of branding until you couldn't tell critique from product. The common thread isn't a technique. It's a stance: circulation, screenshots, and platform life treated as material.
