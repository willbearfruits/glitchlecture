Bedrooms & Demoscene

1980s

Glitch moves from expensive media labs to bedrooms, hackers and cheap computers. The signal art of the studio gets re-grown in teenage hands, on machines that cost a fraction of a video synthesiser and shipped to millions of homes.

demosceneBBS / ANSIcircuit bending

Glitch leaves the lab

For two decades the people bending the signal were the people who could afford the signal: artists with residencies, broadcasters, university media centres, anyone with access to a video synthesiser or a room full of oscilloscopes. The 1980s break that monopoly. A home computer is cheap, it is everywhere, and it lands in bedrooms full of teenagers with nothing but time. The result is a screen culture grown out of piracy, hacking and weird obsession — and it is here that glitch stops being an institutional gesture and becomes a folk practice.

This era runs on copied software, dial-up phone lines and machines pushed far past anything their manuals admit. It is anti-corporate by instinct: the people who knew these computers best were rarely the people the manufacturers were selling to. Out of that scene comes a whole grammar of screen aesthetics — and a direct ancestor of net.art, which is still a decade away.

The demoscene

The demoscene grew straight out of software cracking. When a group broke the copy protection on a game, they would sign their work — first with a static "crack screen", then with an intro bolted onto the front of the copied software: a cracktro. These intros were bragging rights. Scrolling greetings, music, animation, all squeezed into the few kilobytes you could spare without bloating the pirated file. The crack was the product; the cracktro was the signature.

Then the signatures got better than the cracks. Coders, musicians and pixel artists started making the intros for their own sake, then cutting the software loose entirely. What remained were demos: standalone, real-time audiovisual programs whose only purpose was to show off what a machine could do under severe constraints — fixed memory, fixed CPU, no help from a fast graphics card you didn't have.

Mastery, not failure

This is the crucial distinction. Most of the history on this site is about embracing error — letting the signal break and calling the break beautiful. The demoscene is the opposite impulse: it celebrates total mastery, machines pushed flawlessly past their expected use. So it sits adjacent to glitch, not inside it. They share a soil — low-level machine knowledge, hardware abuse, pixel aesthetics, real-time AV coding, ruthless compression tricks, low-res beauty, and a deep anti-corporate computer culture — but the demoscene wants to win where glitch wants to fail. Both are art made by people who know the metal better than the people who built it.

That cultural weight has since been recognised: the demoscene has been entered as digital cultural heritage in UNESCO-related national contexts — one of the first born-digital, born-amateur practices to be archived as heritage rather than nostalgia.

BBS, ANSI and the soil for net.art

The demoscene didn't happen in isolation. It shared phone lines with a whole bulletin-board culture. Before the web, you connected your modem to a Bulletin Board System — someone else's computer, often in someone else's bedroom — to trade files, read messages and lurk. This was a text world, and text became a visual medium.

ASCII culture turned plain characters into pictures. ANSI art went further, using terminal colour and block characters to build elaborate scene logos and login screens that loaded line by line, almost animated by the speed of the modem. Around it grew tracker music (songs built from sampled instruments arranged in a grid), the warez and shareware economies, and a deep love of the terminal as an aesthetic rather than just a tool. All of it — the groups, the greetings, the gift economy of copied files — is the soil that net.art will grow in once the network goes public.

Circuit bending

Not everything in this era happened on screen. Circuit bending is the hardware half of bedroom glitch: physically short-circuiting toys, cheap keyboards and other consumer electronics to coax out sounds and images they were never designed to make. You open a speaking toy, you bridge two points on the board with a wet finger or a wire, and you listen for the moment it breaks in an interesting way. There is no manual. The instrument is found by abuse.

It is the perfect companion to the demoscene's software wizardry: same anti-corporate streak, same refusal to use a machine the way the box says, but aimed at unpredictability instead of control. It is also the cleanest physical metaphor for what comes later — databending, the deliberate corruption of files, is simply circuit bending for data. Bridge the wrong bytes, listen for the beautiful break.
