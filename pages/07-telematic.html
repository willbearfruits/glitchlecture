Networks Before the Web

1983–1993

Before the Web was normal, artists were already making art out of networks. No browsers, no homepages, no hyperlinks the way you know them — just modems, mailing lists and a hunch that the wire itself could be the work.

The wire as medium

We tend to date "internet art" from the Web, as if nothing happened online until pages had backgrounds. Wrong. For a full decade before that, artists were treating the network not as a place to put art but as the material art is made from. The transmission, the latency, the noise on the line — that was the medium.

The clearest early statement is Roy Ascott's La Plissure du Texte (1983). Ascott used computer networking to run a collaborative fairy tale across participants in different countries, each one assigned a role and writing into a shared, distributed story. There was no single author and no central manuscript — the text existed only as the sum of what the network passed around. Ascott called this telematic art: authorship dissolved into the connection itself. It is, if you squint, the whole thesis of the next forty years arriving early.

Networked transmission is lossy. Corruption rising through a phrase — a foreshadow of the famous garbled email that gave net.art its name.

Communities on the wire

The Thing: a BBS for art and theory

Networked authorship needs somewhere to live, and in 1991 Wolfgang Staehle gave it an address. The Thing, founded in New York, began as a bulletin board system — a BBS — devoted to contemporary art and cultural theory. You dialled in, you read, you argued, you posted. Before the Web flattened everything into pages, The Thing grew into one of the most important early online art communities: a genuine scene, with critics and artists talking in real time over phone lines.

This matters because it kills the myth that the early net was empty. The infrastructure of online art — the forums, the flame wars, the in-jokes, the gatekeeping — was being built by artists who saw the network as a social space, not a billboard.

Cyberfeminism: against the neutral machine

Also in 1991, the Australian collective VNS Matrix released A Cyberfeminist Manifesto for the 21st Century. The dominant fantasy of cyberspace then — and, let's be honest, now — was that it was a clean, neutral, frictionless frontier. Cyberfeminism called that what it was: a masculine, technocratic daydream, sold as objectivity. VNS Matrix attacked the idea that the machine had no gender and no body, and insisted the network was political all the way down. If the wire is a medium, then who controls the wire is the question.

Everything that already existed

Here is the point to take away: before "net.art" had its name, the practice was already fully formed. The famous garbled email that supposedly produced the term arrived late. By then the field had: networked writing, BBS communities, cyberfeminism, tactical media, mailing lists, ASCII culture, online identity play and the first crude browser experiments. The name was a label slapped on a movement that had been transmitting for ten years.
