GLI.TC/H & Dirty New Media

2010–2016

First it is shocking and critical. Then it becomes a filter. Then it becomes a commercial style. This is the decade glitch art grows up — festivals, theory, a manifesto — and gets eaten by the thing it was attacking.

Menkmantheory & festivalsdomestication

A scene with enough mass to gather

By 2010 the loose, distributed practice of breaking digital media had accumulated something it didn't have before: community, theory and a working body of practice all at once. There were enough people doing this, talking about this, and arguing about this that they could stop being scattered forum threads and net.art experiments and actually meet in a room. The error had a public.

That public organised itself around festivals. GLI.TC/H began in Chicago in 2010 as a free and open conference-festival-gathering — a hybrid form that refused to choose between the academic panel, the noise gig and the hackerspace workshop. It was built around noise, new media, failure, systems and glitches, and it pulled together the people who were defining the field: Rosa Menkman, Jon Satrom, Nick Briz, Evan Meaney and many others. The point was not to crown a canon. The point was to keep the practice open, communal and slightly ungovernable — to gather around the broken thing without sanding it smooth.

The theory: a moment, and a warning

The decade's central theory comes from Rosa Menkman. The Glitch Moment(um) and the Glitch Studies Manifesto gave the field a vocabulary for what it had been doing on instinct — the difference between a real break in the medium and a picture of one, the politics of letting a system show its own limits, the fleeting "moment" when an error is still legible as error before it gets normalised.

Domestication

But the part that matters most here is the warning. Menkman saw the trajectory before it finished happening. A glitch is first shocking and critical — it interrupts, it exposes the machine, it refuses to behave. Then it becomes a Photoshop filter — a one-click look anyone can apply to anything. Then it becomes a commercial style — a mood, a brand, a music-video texture sold back to you. This is domestication: the moment a critical gesture is captured, made safe, and turned into decoration that knows nothing about the medium it imitates.

It's the difference between breaking a window and printing a photo of a broken window on a t-shirt.

Dirty New Media

If Menkman gave the field its warning, Jon Cates gave it an attitude. Dirty New Media is a direct rejection of the clean, corporate look of mainstream digital design — the frictionless gradients, the rounded rectangles, the seamless render that pretends computation has no cost. Cates foregrounds the opposite: media that is messy, unstable, imperfect and lived. Technological systems that show their seams, their crashes, their accumulated grime. Not glitch as a luxury veneer, but glitch as honesty about what machines actually are when you use them hard.

"Dirty" is the operative word. It's the dirt under the fingernails of a real practice — against the antiseptic, the brand-safe, the demo-ready. It says the lived computer is a dirty computer, and that's where the truth is.

A contradictory decade

The 2010s pull in opposite directions at the same time, and you have to hold all of it at once. Glitch becomes more visible — it's in advertising, in fashion, in your phone's camera app. It becomes more theorised — manifestos, festivals, university courses. It becomes far easier to fake — one slider does what used to take a hex editor and a ruined afternoon. It becomes more commercial — a sellable aesthetic. And, despite all of that, in the right hands it becomes politically sharper, precisely because the practitioners now understand exactly what's being taken from them.

So the field doesn't simply "win" or "sell out." It does both, simultaneously, and the interesting work in this era is the work that knows the difference.

The counter-move

That's where the resistance lives — not in refusing visibility, but in keeping the practice real, open and dangerous. Willbear's answer is two-part. Serious S.H.I.T. and The Rabbit Hole are the open, communal layer: workshops and "creative destruction" tools that keep the knowledge free and the door open, in the GLI.TC/H spirit of gathering rather than gatekeeping. And WRONG is the technical refusal: genuine byte-level corruption, not a filter — a tool that actually breaks the file, the way Menkman insisted real glitch must, instead of painting a flattering picture of damage that never happened.
