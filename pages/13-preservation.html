Preserving Net Art

2001–2019

When the work is a behaviour inside a technical environment, how do you keep it alive? Net art doesn't sit still to be photographed — it runs, breaks, and depends on the machine around it.

The institutions catch up

For a long time the net.art crowd treated the museum as the enemy — the white cube was exactly the system they were routing around. But by the 2000s and 2010s the institutions stopped pretending the work didn't exist and started taking internet art seriously. Not as decoration on a website, but as a body of art history that needed to be commissioned, shown, and kept.

The Whitney's artport, launched in 2001, was the first big move: a museum portal and online gallery dedicated to net-art commissions. A real institution, with a real budget, treating a browser window as a gallery wall. That's a quiet revolution. It said the work belonged in the canon — and, crucially, that the canon now had to live online, where it could rot.

Rhizome went further. Its Net Art Anthology, launched in 2016 and completed in 2019, presented and restored 100 important works from net-art history — a deliberate attempt to write a chronology and, in the same gesture, to rescue the works from disappearing while you read about them. Curation and conservation became the same job.

The preservation problem

Here's why this is hard, and why it's not the same as hanging a painting. Net art has a preservation problem baked into its body. Browsers change. Flash dies. Plugins disappear. Servers vanish when the rent stops getting paid. Links rot. Screen sizes shift, operating systems move on, the APIs a piece called out to get switched off without warning.

And the deeper issue: the work was never just an "image." It was a behaviour inside a technical environment. A net.art piece is what happens when a specific browser, on a specific operating system, with a specific plugin, tries to render a specific pile of code over a specific connection. Take a screenshot and you've kept a corpse, not the animal. You've preserved the output and thrown away the thing that made it.

Every copy degrades. Every server eventually returns a 404. This is the steady entropy that conservators are fighting against — not dramatic destruction, just the slow accumulation of broken dependencies until one day the page is gone.

Keeping a behaviour alive

So preservation stops being a storage problem and becomes part of art history itself — a creative, interpretive act. To keep net art alive you usually reach for one of four moves, often several at once:

  • Emulation — run the work inside a faithful copy of its original technical environment. Rhizome's Webrecorder captures a page's live behaviour, and oldweb.today lets you load a site through an emulated period browser, so a 1999 piece can be seen the way 1999 saw it.
  • Reconstruction — rebuild the work from the code and documentation when the original can't run any more, accepting that the rebuild is a translation.
  • Documentation — record what the piece did, how it behaved, what it depended on, so that even a dead work leaves a usable trace.
  • Reinterpretation — port the work to a new environment and admit, openly, that you've changed it. Sometimes the honest move is to migrate the behaviour, not the bytes.

None of these is neutral. Each one is a decision about what the work was — was it the look, the code, the experience, or the friction? The conservator becomes a co-author, and the version you see in 2019 of a piece from 2001 is a reading as much as a recording.

Decay is the condition, not the bug

Here's the turn. We talk about obsolescence as if it were a flaw — a failure of the institutions, the formats, the artists for not building things to last. But decay and obsolescence aren't bugs of digital art. They are its condition. The medium is made of churn. A file is only ever a temporary arrangement that the next system update can refuse to read.

Glitch art has been telling us this the whole way down: the failure of the medium is the truth of the medium. Net art just makes the timescale visible. The work doesn't fail because someone was careless; it fails because that's what living inside a technical environment means. Nothing here survives uncorrupted.

Which makes the conservator's work strangely heroic and a little absurd at the same time — staffing a hospice for art that was always, by design, going to die. The Net Art Anthology isn't a vault. It's a record of a fight against entropy that you cannot win, only postpone, and the postponement is the point.
