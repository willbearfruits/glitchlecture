The Aesthetics of Failure

1990s–2000

The failures of digital technology became the raw material of music. While the visual artists were tearing images apart, the musicians were doing exactly the same thing to sound — and they got there first with a theory.

The siblings in the signal

Everything you have read about so far on this site happened to pictures. Magnet TV, datamosh, pixel sorting, net.art — images and video, broken on purpose. But there was a parallel movement, running on the same wires at the same time, and it broke sound instead. Visual glitch art and glitch music are not cousins. They are siblings. They were born from the same realisation: that the machine has a body, the body fails, and the failure is interesting.

This page is the bridge. If you came here from the image chapters, this is where you find out that the people listening to clipped, stuttering, error-ridden audio were thinking the exact same thoughts as the people staring at corrupted JPEGs. And if you are a musician, this is your chapter specifically — because the rest of the lecture is about pictures, but the tools at the bottom of this page are pedals you can plug a guitar into.

Oval, and the sound of the format

In the early-to-mid 1990s, a German project called Oval began making music out of the things a CD player was never supposed to let you hear. They worked with CD skipping, scratches, loops and raw digital audio errors — not as accidents to be edited out, but as the actual material of the composition. Where a producer would reach for the undo button, Oval reached for the wounded disc.

Their record 94diskont (1995) is the canonical statement. Pitchfork describes it as an album built on CD skips and errors — music that makes you hear the CD format itself, rather than whatever the CD was nominally supposed to play. The medium stops being a transparent window onto the music and becomes the music. The skip is not interrupting the song. The skip is the song.

This is the same move the visual artists make when they show you the compression block instead of the photograph. The system exposed. The container made audible.

Cascone writes it down

A movement needs a manifesto, and in 2000 it got one. Kim Cascone's essay The Aesthetics of Failure: "Post-Digital" Tendencies in Contemporary Computer Music is the major theory text for this whole field. Cascone argued, plainly, that the failures of digital technology had become raw material for contemporary computer music.

Read that list of failures slowly, because it is the entire vocabulary of glitch in eight words: glitches, clipping, bugs, noise, crashes. The things the engineer spent decades trying to suppress — the residue of the machine working badly — were now the source. Cascone's word for the era was post-digital: a moment when digital tools were so ubiquitous and so transparent that the only interesting thing left about them was where they broke.

The words this movement gives us

If glitch art has a shared dialect, a lot of it is minted here. You will hear these terms across every chapter that follows:

  • failure — not a bug to fix, a feature to amplify.
  • post-digital — past the novelty of the digital, interested only in its seams.
  • artifact — the visible/audible trace left by a process going wrong.
  • compression & the noise floor — the hiss and blocks underneath everything.
  • error as material — the crash treated like clay.
  • machine residue — what the system leaves behind when it fails — the system exposed.

A whole scene, not one record

Oval and Cascone are the headline acts, but they sat inside a wider practice. Yasunao Tone was scoring and "wounding" CDs — preparing the digital media physically so the player would misread it, an heir to the prepared-piano tradition dragged into the laser age. Nicolas Collins was hacking and circuit-bending electronics, building instruments out of misused consumer hardware. And the German label Mille Plateaux gathered the whole tendency under a name with its Clicks & Cuts series — the moment the genre stopped being scattered experiments and got a label, a roster and a flag.

Clicks. Cuts. The tiniest possible sonic events — a single digital tick, a sliced-off fragment — arranged into rhythm and texture. The dust of the medium, swept into a beat.
