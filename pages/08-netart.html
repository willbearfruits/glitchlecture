net.art

1994–1999

The internet is not a gallery. The internet is the material. A loose, argumentative pack of artists pointed at the web itself — links, browsers, broken pages, addresses, servers, spam — and called it the work.

A name that arrived as junk text

From about 1994 a scattered group of artists started using the internet not as a place to show art but as the stuff art was made from: Vuk Ćosić, JODI, Alexei Shulgin, Olia Lialina, Heath Bunting, Rachel Baker. No gallery, no white wall, no object you could insure. The medium was the network and everything that came with it — protocols, servers, addresses, the browser, and all the ways those things go wrong.

The label itself has a perfect origin story, and it is a glitch. As Artforum tells it, the term net.art came out of a corrupted email that landed in Vuk Ćosić's inbox in 1995. The message was junk — mangled, unreadable — except for one fragment that survived legible in the noise: net.art. The movement that would spend five years insisting that errors and broken machines could be art was, fittingly, named by an error in a broken machine. net.art is literally born from a glitch.

JODI: the browser as a composed malfunction

If one practice sits at the centre of this, it's JODI — the duo Joan Heemskerk and Dirk Paesmans. They treated the browser, HTML, the source code, broken layouts, error-like interfaces and your own confusion as their materials. You'd land on one of their pages and it would look like something had gone catastrophically wrong: flashing text, garbage characters, a layout collapsing in on itself, a screen that seemed to be malfunctioning at you.

The trick — the thing that makes it art and not just a bad page — is that it's composed. Nothing is actually broken. The chaos is authored, frame by frame, down to the markup. View the source and you find another work entirely: ASCII diagrams and hidden structure that the browser renders as noise. Rhizome lists JODI's Automatic Rain (1995) among the early pieces; the wider body of work lived at the unforgettable address wwwwwwwww.jodi.org. The lesson is the one this whole movement turns on: the page looks wrong on purpose, and the wrongness is the point.

Move the mouse and click. Glyph-storms, rogue blinking buttons (a nod to Shulgin’s Form Art) and source-code debris — the browser as a composed malfunction.

Frames, widgets and phone booths

Lialina — frames as cinema

Olia Lialina's My Boyfriend Came Back from the War (1996) is the canonical browser narrative. It tells a fractured story of return and absence using nothing but the raw furniture of the early web: HTML frames that split and subdivide as you click, hypertext fragments, black space, the act of navigation itself becoming a kind of editing. You don't watch it — you click your way through it, and the frameset does the work a film cut would do. Cinema, made of <frameset>.

Shulgin — UI as composition

Alexei Shulgin's Form Art (1997) took the most boring possible material — the grey widgets of an HTML form — and made them the entire palette. Buttons, checkboxes, text fields, radio buttons and dropdowns, arranged purely as visual composition. The default interface chrome of the web, the stuff you click past without seeing, pulled into the foreground and treated as line, mass and rhythm.

Bunting — tactical, networked event

Heath Bunting's King's Cross Phone-In (1994) shows the movement's other edge — tactical media. He published the numbers of the public phone booths inside London's King's Cross station and used mailing lists to invite people to call them, all at once, at a set time. For a moment a train station became a networked instrument: ringing booths, confused commuters, strangers picking up. The internet reaching out and physically rearranging a place. The network as event, not image.

The internet as material

Pull it together and the thesis is blunt. The internet is not a gallery you hang work inside — it is the material you carve. Links, browsers, broken pages, addresses, servers, protocols, spam, identity, confusion: every one of those is a substance you can shape, jam or corrupt. net.art took the connective tissue of the web and made it the subject and the medium at once. That's the inheritance — and it didn't end in 1999.

The heir, live

JODI built a page that was wrong on purpose. Willbear's WRONG takes that logic and turns the whole browser into the artwork — a browser that corrupts what it shows, byte-flipping images before the decoder sees them and pouring noise into text.
