The Magnet on the CRT

1963–1969

Paik makes the television reveal itself. The image becomes a wound in the system.

Nam June PaikMagnet TVvideo art is born

The set turned against itself

Until now the television was infrastructure. It pointed one way: a broadcaster spoke, a living room listened, and the picture on the glass was somebody else's decision arriving on schedule. Nam June Paik's whole move is to refuse that one-way street — to grab the apparatus by the throat and force it to show you what it actually is, which is electrons being thrown at a phosphor screen by a magnetic field. Tate describes his work plainly as turning televisions "against themselves." That phrase is the entire era.

In 1963 Paik staged Exposition of Music — Electronic Television in Wuppertal, scattering prepared and altered TV sets through a house and distorting the images they carried — some of them, Tate notes, warped with magnets. This is the first time anyone treats the television set itself as raw material rather than a delivery mechanism. The content stops mattering. The medium becomes the work.

1965: a magnet, a black-and-white set, and the birth of induced glitch

Two years later the gesture sharpens into a single unforgettable object. Magnet TV (1965) is a black-and-white television with a large industrial magnet sitting on top of it. That's the whole piece. The magnet's field reaches inside the tube and bends the electron beam off its disciplined raster, so whatever the set is "receiving" collapses into a writhing, abstract, moving form. Broadcast becomes pure shape. The Whitney calls it an early example of prepared television — the explicit analogy is John Cage's prepared piano, where you wedge objects between the strings to make the instrument do something it was never built to do. Paik does the same thing, but to mass media.

The logic is brutally clean and entirely analog. The television is supposed to deliver content. Paik makes it reveal itself instead. And the magnet is not a metaphor on a screen — it is a physical force acting on physical electrons. There is no signal to "hack," no file to corrupt. You just hold a field next to the hardware and the obedient image tears open. The picture becomes, exactly, a wound in the system.

Why this is the canonical proto-glitch moment

If you want the cleanest possible definition of induced glitch — error deliberately provoked in a working system — this is it. Nothing here is broken in the ordinary sense. The set still works; the magnet still works; the broadcast is still arriving. The artist simply introduces a force the engineers never accounted for and lets the machine fail beautifully and on purpose. Every later move in this lecture — datamoshing, databending, pixel sorting, feedback — is a descendant of one man deciding the failure mode was the point.

Crucially, Paik isn't destroying the television out of contempt. He's destroying its authority. The same glass that hands you the evening news on someone else's terms can, with one cheap magnet, be made to answer to a person in a room. That is the creative-destruction thesis in its earliest pure form: you wreck the intended function to expose, and to seize, the medium underneath.

1969: video art arrives

The experiment becomes a field. In 1969 the Howard Wise Gallery in New York mounted "TV as a Creative Medium" — a landmark show that did exactly what its title says, establishing television and video as an artistic medium in their own right rather than as broadcast plumbing. After Wuppertal and the magnet, this is the institutional moment where the idea lands in the United States and sticks. From here on, the screen is a place artists work, not just a thing artists are shown on.
