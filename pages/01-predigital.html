Damage, Chance & Noise

1910s–1950s

Before the computer, artists were already attacking and misusing media. Long before a single bit was flipped, painters scratched film, composers framed silence, and provocateurs handed the work over to accident. This is where creative destruction begins.

direct filmchancenoise as material

The mistake was always the medium

Glitch art did not arrive with the JPEG. The reflex behind it — treating noise, damage and accident as raw material rather than as failure to be hidden — is at least a century old. The interesting move is not the digital corruption itself; it is the decision that the corruption is worth looking at. That decision was made, repeatedly, by artists working in paint, in film emulsion, in sound, decades before anyone owned a hard drive.

Frame what follows as ancestors, not as glitch art proper. These people were not glitching anything. But they built the conceptual permission slip that later digital artists would cash in: the idea that the surface, the tool, the error and the unwanted by-product are not obstacles to the art — they are the art.

Experimental film: attacking the strip

Len Lye — the strip as image

Len Lye worked the film strip itself. Instead of pointing a camera at the world, he painted, scratched and stencilled directly onto the celluloid — no lens, no exposure, no capture. The physical strip becomes the image. In a single gesture the medium stops being transparent: you are no longer looking through the film at a recorded scene, you are looking at the film as a marked, scarred, handmade object. That refusal of transparency — making the carrier visible — is the whole game later glitch art plays with compression artefacts and dropped frames.

Stan Brakhage — Mothlight, no camera

Stan Brakhage pushed the same logic to its limit with Mothlight (1963). He made it with no camera at all, pressing moth wings, flower petals and blades of grass between strips of film. The normal tool-chain — lens, light, emulsion, capture — is broken, and the material process becomes the image directly. The dead moth is not a picture of a moth; it is the moth, lit by the projector. When you remove the expected apparatus and let the raw material speak, you get exactly the disorientation that a corrupted file produces: a thing that should have been mediated, presented instead as brute matter.

Sound & chance: framing the unwanted

John Cage — 4′33″ and the permission slip

John Cage moved the attention elsewhere entirely — to chance, to silence, to ambient sound, to the unwanted. 4′33″ (1952) is not glitch music; nothing is corrupted, nothing breaks. The performer sits and plays nothing for four minutes and thirty-three seconds, and the piece becomes whatever you hear in that frame: the room, the coughs, the traffic, the hum. What Cage granted was conceptual permission — to treat noise, accident and context as composition rather than as interference to be edited out.

That permission is the hinge of this whole story. Once the "unwanted" sound is admitted as material, the door is open for clicks, skips and crushed bits to be admitted too. Cage didn't open the door to the glitch directly, but he proved the door existed.

Dada and the Futurists — noise first

And he wasn't first. The Futurists made noise itself a subject, building instruments to play the sounds of the industrial city. Dada turned chance into method — letting accident, randomness and the absurd decide the work, deliberately handing control over to the very forces an artist is supposed to master. Between them they had already established that noise and chance were legitimate art materials. The twentieth century kept rediscovering this idea; the computer just gave it new surfaces to ruin.

noise as material
Cage opened the door to noise; here the door is a CD-skip / clicks-and-cuts / bitcrush engine. Click play, then mangle. The unwanted sound as the composition.

Why this is the root

Pull it together and one sentence lands: before the computer there was already a tradition that said the mistake, the surface, the noise, the tool, the accident — that is the art. Strip away the technology and creative destruction is not a digital trick at all. It is an attitude toward materials that predates the machine by half a century, and these film-scratchers, silence-framers and noise-makers are its deepest root.
