Electronic Images

1950s–1960s

The screen stops being photographic and becomes signal, code, math. Before there was a file to corrupt, there was a waveform to photograph and an algorithm to run.

The image leaves the lens

Everything in the previous chapter still answered to a camera. Light hit a surface, a chemical or a sensor recorded it, and the result was a picture of something. This era is where that contract breaks. The image stops being a recording of the world and starts being a recording of a process — an electrical signal, a set of instructions, a machine doing what its logic forces it to do.

That shift matters because it is the substrate everything later sits on. You cannot glitch a file until the image is a file. You cannot bend a signal until the picture is a signal. The work in this period builds that foundation, usually without anyone calling it "glitch," and usually decades before the tools we now take for granted existed.

Ben Laposky: drawing with pure signal

Start with the cleanest example of the idea. In the early 1950s, Ben Laposky pointed a camera at an oscilloscope and photographed the glowing waveforms on its screen. He called the results Oscillons — abstract images made entirely from electronic signal, exhibited as art at a time when most people only saw a scope as test equipment.

There is no Photoshop here, no file, no corruption. There is voltage shaped into curves, a phosphor screen, and a long exposure. But the principle is already complete: an electrical signal turned into an abstract image. Laposky was, in effect, drawing with the machine's own behaviour — tuning oscillators the way a photographer might frame a shot. It is the first time the picture is purely a trace of electronics, and nothing else.

The plotter and the algorithm

The 1960s and 70s push the idea one step further. Now the machine is not just producing the signal — it is following instructions. This is the first wave of computer and plotter art, and it is where software, algorithm and machine logic become artistic material in their own right.

The names to know: Frieder Nake, who made some of the earliest generative plotter drawings; Vera Molnar, whose algorithmic compositions let chance and the machine share authorship; Manfred Mohr, working systematically through programmed geometry; Harold Cohen, who spent decades teaching a program to draw; and the early computer-image and animation work of Charles Csuri and Lillian Schwartz. None of this is "glitch" in any literal sense. What it establishes is bigger than glitch: that a computational limitation — the grid, the pen, the rounding error, the rule that has to be obeyed — can be the point of the work rather than a problem to hide.

Molnar's term for her method was désordre: disorder, introduced deliberately and incrementally, so that the rigid machine grid drifts and breaks under controlled randomness. That is the seed of an entire attitude — let the system misbehave on purpose, and call the misbehaviour the art.

Why this counts as a beginning

It is tempting to treat early computer art as a footnote — quaint plotter doodles before the real thing arrived. The institutions disagree. The V&A frames the computer art of the 1960s and 70s as a major precursor to contemporary digital art, not a curiosity off to the side. That framing is correct, and it is the whole reason this chapter exists.

Here is the meaning to carry forward. Across roughly two decades, the image quietly stops being only photographic. It becomes signal in Laposky's oscillons, and it becomes code, math and machine behaviour in the plotter generation. Once the picture is made of those things, it can be tuned, perturbed, mis-instructed and broken — and that is exactly what every later glitch artist will do. The break has not happened yet. But the material that can break has just been invented.
