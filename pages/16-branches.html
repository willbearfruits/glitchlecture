A family, not a movement

By now you have read the chapters one at a time, in order, like a history. But history is a lie we tell to make things sit still. None of this happened in a line. Video artists, code artists, circuit benders, net.artists and laptop musicians were all elbow-deep in the same machine at the same time, asking the same question with different tools. Glitch art is not a single river. It is a watershed — a whole family of practices that drain into one idea.

This page is the map. Below are the branches that sit closest to glitch art, the canon of names worth committing to memory, and then — at the end — the distinction that decides whether any given broken thing is worth your time. Use it as an index. Use it as a reading list. Use it to find the room you actually belong in.

The branches

Each of these is a discipline in its own right, with its own histories and heroes. Here they are as a single field, ordered roughly by how close their blood runs to glitch.

Video art

The closest ancestor. Nam June Paik and the Vasulkas treated the television and the video signal as a substance to be bent, magnetised and synthesised. Glitch art inherits its central instinct from here: the image is electronic, and electronics can be made to misbehave on purpose.

Computer / algorithmic art

Code, procedure, the generative, the question of machine authorship. When the artwork is a program rather than a picture, the "artist" partly becomes the system itself — and the bug becomes a co-author.

Demoscene

Mastery of constraints. Squeezing impossible audiovisuals out of fixed, tiny hardware — and in doing so exposing exactly what the chip, the memory and the compression are really doing. Glitch's disciplined, show-off cousin.

Circuit bending

Short-circuiting consumer electronics by chance and by wire — making toys and keyboards sing wrong. Databending is its file-level equivalent: you do to a JPEG with a hex editor what the bender does to a Speak & Spell with a screwdriver.

Glitch music

The aesthetics of failure in sound. Oval and Yasunao Tone as practice, Kim Cascone as theory, Mille Plateaux as the label that named it. The audible sibling of the corrupted image.

net.art

The browser, raw HTML, the broken interface and the logic of the network as material. Art that lives in the page itself and treats the error, the dead link and the wrong layout as the medium.

Software art

The program as the artwork — not its output. Bugs, user interfaces, executables and code poetry. The source listing is the canvas; running it is optional.

Tactical media / hacktivism

Turning media systems against themselves. Activist, often anonymous, using the network's own infrastructure as a weapon and a stage. Glitch with a target.

Dirty new media

A deliberate stand against clean, frictionless, corporate digital. Messy, glitchy, lo-fi, hand-soldered — beauty in the seams the brands sand off.

Post-internet

Art made after the internet stopped being a place you visit and became the default condition of everything. The network is no longer the subject; it is the air.

Vaporwave / Y2K / web nostalgia

Compression artefacts, obsolete interfaces and the ruins of corporate digital, recycled as mood. The wreckage of one era's optimism made into the next era's aesthetic.

AI glitch

The newest branch: the failure of synthetic meaning. Where the machine no longer just renders wrong — it hallucinates, melts and confabulates, and the artefact is a crack in meaning itself.

The canon — who to know

Names worth carrying. Grouped by branch, ordered roughly the way the history runs. One line each — enough to know where to look next, not enough to do the looking for you.

Proto / pre-glitch

Len Lye — direct / scratched film, drawn straight onto the celluloid.
Stan Brakhage — Mothlight — physical matter pressed into the film strip.
John Cage — chance, noise and silence as legitimate material.
Ben Laposky — oscilloscope abstractions — electrons drawn as art.

Video / signal

Nam June Paik — Magnet TV, prepared TVs, the Paik/Abe synthesiser.
Steina & Woody Vasulka — video understood as raw, manipulable signal.
Shigeko Kubota — video sculpture and the moving image as object.
Dara Birnbaum — broadcast television cut against itself.
Gary Hill — image, language and electronic signal entwined.

Computer / systems

Vera Molnár — algorithmic drawing and controlled disorder.
Frieder Nake — early computer-generated graphics with a theory behind them.
Manfred Mohr — the cube and the line, drawn by code for decades.
Harold Cohen — AARON, a program that made its own pictures.
Lillian Schwartz — computer film and image at Bell Labs.
Charles Csuri — a painter who turned the computer into a brush.

JODI — the browser made monstrous; the page as breakdown.
Vuk Ćosić — ASCII cinema and the very term "net.art".

Heath Bunting — infrastructure, links and the net as public space.

Rachel Baker — data, loyalty and the surveillance of the network.
Electronic Disturbance Theater — the virtual sit-in; tactical media in action.
Eva & Franco Mattes — hoaxes, identity and the net's blurred reality.

Glitch / databending / post-glitch

Rosa Menkman — the Glitch Studies Manifesto; the field's theorist.

Kim Asendorf — pixel sorting and procedural corruption.

Datamosh / compression

Takeshi Murata — datamosh as lush, melting video painting.
Paper Rad — maximalist net-trash and GIF culture.
Paul B. Davis — codec abuse and the politics of the medium.

Post-internet / platform

The distinction

If you take one tool from this whole lecture, take this one. There are three ways a glitch can arrive, and they are not equal.

  • Accidental — the machine genuinely failed. The corruption is real, found, unrepeatable. You caught the system in the act.
  • Induced — you reached in and broke it on purpose: flipped the byte, pulled the keyframe, scored the disc. The failure is real, but you authored the conditions.
  • Simulated — the glitch is faked. A filter, a preset, a "VHS look" with no actual relationship to any real failure. The costume of brokenness over a perfectly healthy file.

None of these is forbidden. But the strongest work has a real relationship to the structure of its medium — it breaks the thing it is actually made of, in a way that thing can actually break. A simulated glitch can be beautiful, but it is decoration. An induced or accidental one is an argument. The difference is whether the wound is honest.

The thesis

Glitch art is not a style. It is a way of asking what the machine is hiding.the whole lecture, in one line

That is the entire field, compressed. Not a look you apply, but a question you ask — of the codec, the chip, the protocol, the model. And this whole site is that idea, built: every chapter a different machine, every demo a different way of asking, every tool below a different answer. The lecture ends here. The asking does not.
