1969–1979

After Paik, the image gets sculpted

Paik dragged a magnet across the face of a television and proved the picture was not sacred — it was a field you could push. The generation that followed took that lesson and ran. If the electronic image bends, then you can shape it. And if you can shape it, the video frame is no longer a window you point at the world. It becomes raw material — something you build, route, and destroy on purpose.

This is the decade where video artists stop being camera operators and start being instrument builders. They want control of the signal itself, in real time, at the level of voltage and scan line. Nobody is content to record reality and play it back anymore. The whole point is to intervene before the image even settles.

The Paik/Abe Video Synthesizer

In 1969–71 Nam June Paik worked with the engineer Shuya Abe to build the Paik/Abe Video Synthesizer: a machine for real-time manipulation and mixing of multiple video sources. Instead of editing footage after the fact, an artist could colourise, layer, and warp live inputs as they happened — a path straight toward live electronic image processing as a performance medium. The synthesizer made the deeper claim explicit. Video was now something you played, like an instrument, not something you simply shot.

The Vasulkas: video as pure signal

Steina and Woody Vasulka are the people who said the quiet part out loud. They treated video as pure signal — an electronic waveform with its own behaviour, its own grain, its own logic. Their founding question is a punk one: why should video have to imitate the human eye at all? A camera is just a habit. The signal does not owe anybody a picture of a face or a tree.

So they built tools for direct signal manipulation rather than camera representation. The interesting object was not the thing in front of the lens; it was the voltage carrying the image, and what happened when you fed that voltage through circuits that bent, split, and re-timed it. The camera became optional. The signal was the subject.

A vocabulary of intervention

Out of this came a whole toolkit of techniques that still describe what we do today — analog video feedback, scan-line distortion, CRT manipulation, video synthesizers, closed-circuit systems, broadcast interruption, early live visuals, and above all signal routing as composition. Patching one output back into another input was not a mistake to be cleaned up. It was the score.

  • Analog feedback — point the signal at itself and watch it spiral into infinite tunnels and bloom.
  • Scan-line distortion — attack the way the beam draws the frame, line by line.
  • Closed-circuit systems — a camera, a monitor, and a loop with no outside world in it.
  • Signal routing as composition — the patch is the artwork; rearranging cables rewrites the piece.

The wider circle

The Vasulkas were not alone. Shigeko Kubota built video into sculpture and turned the moving image into physical, three-dimensional objects. Dara Birnbaum seized broadcast television's own footage and cut it back against itself, exposing how the medium manufactured its images. Gary Hill pushed video toward language and the body, treating the signal as something to think and speak with. Different temperaments, one shared instinct: the electronic image is material, and material can be taken apart.

Why this is the real ancestor of glitch

Here is the move to land. Video stops being an image of the world and becomes a manipulable electronic substance. That single shift is, more than anything else, the direct ancestor of what most people feel when they say "glitch." The tearing, the channel split, the feedback bloom, the sense that the picture is unstable and alive — all of it starts here, in artists who decided the signal mattered more than the subject.
