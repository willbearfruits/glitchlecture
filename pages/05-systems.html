Art + Engineering

1966–1970

Artists stop using technology as a neutral tool and start using it as a messy collaborator — one that talks back, breaks rules, and sometimes draws the picture for you.

E.A.T.cyberneticsmachine as collaborator

The merger goes institutional

The previous eras were artists smuggling machines into the studio. This one is the moment the smuggling stops and the merger becomes official — written down, funded, exhibited, named. Art and technology don't just rub against each other anymore; they incorporate.

The flashpoint is 9 Evenings: Theatre and Engineering in 1966 — a series of performances built out of direct collaboration between artists and engineers from Bell Labs. Out of that collaboration came Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.), founded to do the thing systematically: promote and broker collaboration between artists, engineers, and scientists. Not artists borrowing gear. Artists and engineers in the same room, with the engineer's name on the work.

That sounds bureaucratic. It isn't. It's a structural change in who gets to make art and what counts as a medium. Once you put a Bell Labs engineer on the credits, the circuit becomes part of the artwork — and so does its behavior, its noise, its failure.

the machine as collaborator
A generative system where author and machine share control: you set the disorder, the algorithm draws. Cybernetic Serendipity in one box — re-plot for a new outcome.

Cybernetic Serendipity, 1968

Two years later the question goes international. Cybernetic Serendipity opens at the ICA in London in 1968 and becomes one of the first major exhibitions anywhere to take computer-aided art seriously as art — not a science fair, not a novelty, but a show.

One word in the title does the work

The exhibition pulls together computer-assisted music, poetry, dance, sculpture, animation, and drawing under a single roof. The breadth is the argument: this isn't one weird genre, it's a method that touches everything. And the title says the quiet part out loud — serendipity. The interesting results aren't the ones you specified. They're the ones the system handed you that you didn't ask for.

This is where cybernetics, systems thinking, and feedback enter art for real. A cybernetic system is one that senses its own output and feeds it back into its own behavior. Aim a camera at its own monitor and you get feedback; let an algorithm decide where the pen goes and you get an author who is only half in control. The work stops being a fixed object you made and becomes a process you set running.

Why this is the hinge

This page is deliberately short, because it's a hinge, not a chapter. Behind it sits video art — artists wrestling live signal. Ahead of it sits the home-computer underground, where the machine gets cheap enough to break in your bedroom. This era is the joint between them: the moment the idea gets formalized that the machine is a partner, not a paintbrush.

And that idea is exactly what glitch art inherits. Strip away the institutions and the engineers and you're left with the same question E.A.T. and Cybernetic Serendipity were asking: what happens when artists stop using technology as a neutral tool and start using it as a messy, unpredictable collaborator? The glitch artist who corrupts a JPEG and keeps the accident is answering that question. So is anyone who hands a slider to an algorithm and lets it draw.

the takeaway

Control is something you can share with a system on purpose. The "mistakes" it makes under your loose direction aren't bugs in the artwork — frequently they are the artwork.
